Three-Statement Model: Line Items & Forecast Drivers

Every line item, what it means, and how it's actually forecasted — color-coded by role in the model.

Key driver — the input you set; changing it moves the whole model
Assumption — set as a rate or %, less impactful on its own
Derived — calculated mechanically from other lines, not a direct input

If you only care about a handful of numbers, these are the ones that move a three-statement model

  1. Revenue growth rate — nearly everything else in the model is built as a percentage of revenue, so this one number cascades through all three statements.
  2. Gross margin (COGS as % of revenue) — determines how much of that revenue actually turns into profit.
  3. Operating expense ratio (SG&A + R&D as % of revenue) — the second-biggest swing factor in profitability.
  4. Capex as % of revenue — drives PP&E, which drives depreciation, which drives both net income and cash flow.
  5. Working capital days (DSO, DIO, DPO) — small assumptions that quietly control how much cash gets tied up or freed up each period.
  6. Debt schedule & interest rate — governs interest expense and is the classic source of circularity in a model.

Income Statement

RevenueDriver
Sales for the period.
Forecast: the model's starting point — grown off a historical rate, a unit-economics build (units × price), or a market-share assumption. Almost every other line is expressed as a % of this number.
COGS / Cost of RevenueAssumption
Materials, direct labor, hosting/delivery costs.
Forecast: held as a % of revenue (i.e., target gross margin), not built up from raw inputs, unless you have detailed unit cost data.
Gross ProfitDerived
Revenue − COGS.
Forecast: falls out automatically once revenue and COGS% are set.
SG&AAssumption
Sales, marketing, admin, executive salaries, rent.
Forecast: % of revenue, sometimes with a fixed-cost floor since not all overhead scales with sales.
R&DAssumption
Product development spend.
Forecast: % of revenue, or held flat/grown independently for early-stage companies investing ahead of sales.
D&A (if shown on income statement)Derived
Non-cash expense spreading asset cost over time.
Forecast: not an independent input — it flows out of the PP&E/capex schedule (see balance sheet section). Get capex right and D&A follows.
Operating Income (EBIT)Derived
Gross Profit − Opex.
Forecast: mechanical output of the lines above.
Interest Expense / IncomeDerived
Cost of debt / yield on cash.
Forecast: interest rate assumption × average debt or cash balance from the balance sheet. This is the classic circular reference: interest depends on the debt balance, which depends on cash flow, which depends on net income, which depends on interest. Models handle this with a circularity switch or by using beginning-of-period balances.
Tax RateAssumption
Government's share of pre-tax income.
Forecast: held constant at the company's effective (not statutory) tax rate unless you have specific reason to change it.
Net IncomeDerived
The bottom line.
Forecast: pure output — but it's the single most important output, since it feeds both the balance sheet (retained earnings) and cash flow statement (starting line).

Balance Sheet

Accounts ReceivableAssumption
Cash owed by customers who bought on credit.
Forecast: driven by Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) = AR ÷ Revenue × 365. Set a target DSO, then AR = Revenue × DSO ÷ 365. A small change here has an outsized effect on cash.
InventoryAssumption
Unsold materials/goods.
Forecast: driven by Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) = Inventory ÷ COGS × 365, same mechanic as DSO.
PP&E, netDriver
Buildings, equipment, machinery, net of depreciation.
Forecast: rolls forward as Beginning PP&E + Capex − Depreciation. Capex is usually set as a % of revenue — this single assumption controls PP&E, D&A, and a chunk of the cash flow statement all at once.
Goodwill / IntangiblesStatic
Value from acquisitions, patents, trademarks.
Forecast: usually held flat unless the model assumes future acquisitions or amortizes a specific intangible on a known schedule.
Accounts PayableAssumption
Cash owed to suppliers.
Forecast: driven by Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) = AP ÷ COGS × 365. Higher DPO means the company is holding onto cash longer — good for cash flow, but a real operating constraint, not a free lever.
Accrued Expenses / Deferred RevenueStatic
Costs incurred but unpaid; cash collected but unearned.
Forecast: typically % of revenue or opex, or grown in line with the related driver (e.g., deferred revenue tracks bookings for subscription businesses).
Debt (short- and long-term)Driver
Loans and bonds outstanding.
Forecast: governed by a debt schedule — scheduled repayments, any new issuance, and (in a full model) a revolver that automatically draws or repays based on whether the company has a cash shortfall or surplus. This balance is what interest expense is calculated on.
Retained EarningsDerived
Cumulative profit kept in the business.
Forecast: rolls forward as Beginning Retained Earnings + Net Income − Dividends. Purely mechanical, but it's the line that ties the income statement into the balance sheet.
CashDerived
Cash and equivalents on hand.
Forecast: the "plug" — it's whatever the cash flow statement says ending cash is. This is also what makes the balance sheet balance; if it doesn't, there's an error somewhere upstream.

Cash Flow Statement

Net Income (starting line)Carried over
Pulled directly from the income statement.
+ D&A add-backDerived
Non-cash expense added back.
Forecast: comes straight from the PP&E/capex schedule — no independent assumption needed.
± Working Capital ChangesDerived
Period-over-period change in AR, inventory, AP, accrued items.
Forecast: mechanically calculated from the DSO/DIO/DPO assumptions on the balance sheet — this is where those "small" working capital assumptions actually hit cash.
CapexDriver
Cash spent on PP&E.
Forecast: set directly as % of revenue — one of the biggest single levers in the whole model, since it simultaneously drives investing cash outflow, PP&E, and future D&A.
Acquisitions / Asset SalesScenario-driven
Cash for buying companies or selling assets.
Forecast: usually zero in a base-case forecast unless modeling a specific known deal.
Debt Issued / RepaidDerived
Cash in/out from borrowing or paying down debt.
Forecast: comes from the debt schedule — scheduled amortization plus any revolver draw/paydown needed to keep cash from going negative.
Dividends / BuybacksAssumption
Cash returned to shareholders.
Forecast: set as a payout ratio (% of net income) or a fixed dividend per share, based on stated company policy.
Ending CashDerived
Sum of operating, investing, and financing cash flows plus beginning cash.
Forecast: the final output of the whole model — flows straight back onto the balance sheet as the cash line.

Why this hierarchy matters

A three-statement model has maybe 6–8 real inputs (revenue growth, margins, capex %, working capital days, tax rate, debt/interest terms, payout policy) and dozens of lines that are just mechanical consequences of those inputs. When you're stress-testing a model or sanity-checking someone else's, focus your questions on the driver and assumption lines — the derived lines will take care of themselves if the mechanics are built correctly.
